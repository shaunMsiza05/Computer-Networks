### 1. **Abstract**
This case study investigates a theoretical attack vector in enterprise networks utilizing static NAT without Port Address Translation (No-PAT). It explores how an attacker, upon compromising a firewall or router, could manipulate destination NAT policies to redirect inbound traffic to a malicious internal device. The study simulates the attack logic, evaluates its feasibility, and proposes layered defense mechanisms.

---

### 2. **Background and Motivation**
Static NAT is commonly deployed in enterprise networks to ensure predictable public-to-private IP mappings, especially for services requiring consistent external access. While this design simplifies routing and access control, it introduces a deterministic attack surface. If an attacker compromises the NAT device, they can exploit the rigidity of static mappings to reroute traffic.

---

### 3. **Hypothesis**
If destination NAT policies are independently managed from source NAT, an attacker can poison only the destination NAT table. This allows inbound traffic to be redirected to a single compromised internal device, without disrupting outbound traffic legitimacyâ€”thus maintaining stealth.

| Factor | Impact |

### 6. **Defense Recommendations**
- **Unified NAT Policy Enforcement:** Prevent asymmetric manipulation.
- **NAT Table Integrity Monitoring:** Alert on unexpected remappings.
- **Role-Based Access Control (RBAC):** Limit admin privileges on egress devices.
- **Redundant Firewall Architecture:** Use dual ASA with failover and state sync.
- **Deep Packet Inspection (DPI):** Detect anomalous payloads from spoofed sources.

---

### 7. **Conclusion**
This case study demonstrates that static NAT, while operationally efficient, can be weaponized if destination NAT policies are compromised. Ethical simulation of this attack reveals the importance of policy cohesion, logging integrity, and layered defenses. Future research may explore dynamic NAT poisoning and its implications in cloud-native architectures.
