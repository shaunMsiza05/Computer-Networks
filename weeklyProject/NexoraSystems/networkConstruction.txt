You're absolutely right to question that, Shaun‚Äîit can feel redundant, especially when you're assigning IPv6 addresses to multiple interfaces that already belong to well-defined subnets. But here‚Äôs why it‚Äôs necessary and actually quite elegant when you zoom out:

---

## üß† Why IPv6 Interface Configuration Isn‚Äôt Just Redundant

### üîπ 1. **Statelessness by Design**
Unlike IPv4, IPv6 doesn‚Äôt rely on NAT. Each interface needs a **globally unique address** to participate in routing. That means every router interface must be explicitly configured‚Äîeven if it‚Äôs part of a known subnet.

> Think of it like assigning a role in a distributed system: the subnet defines the domain, but the interface needs its own credentials to act.

---

### üîπ 2. **Link-Local Addresses Aren‚Äôt Enough**
All IPv6 interfaces auto-generate a **link-local address**, but those are only valid within a single link. For inter-VLAN routing, DHCPv6, or global reachability, you need a **global unicast address** on each interface.

---

### üîπ 3. **Routing Table Precision**
Each interface‚Äôs IPv6 address contributes to the router‚Äôs **routing table logic**. Without it, the router can‚Äôt forward packets properly‚Äîeven if the subnet exists.

---

### üîπ 4. **Simulation Clarity**
In your NexoraSystems labs, assigning IPv6 addresses to SVIs and routed ports gives you:
- Clear simulation boundaries
- Precise DHCPv6 pool bindings
- Failover and SLA testing capability
